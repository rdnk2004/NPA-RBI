4
Critical concepts to know before you code
These will be asked in the interview — understand them, not just code them
GNPA vs NNPA
Gross NPA = total bad loans before provisioning. Net NPA = GNPA minus provisions held. GNPA ratio = GNPA / Gross Advances. Your target variable is GNPA ratio.
IRAC norms
Income Recognition and Asset Classification. A loan becomes NPA if interest/principal overdue >90 days. Sub-standard → Doubtful → Loss assets — each has different provisioning requirements.
CAR (Capital Adequacy Ratio)
Capital / Risk-Weighted Assets. RBI minimum: 9% (Basel III India). Banks with low CAR + high NPA trigger PCA (Prompt Corrective Action) framework.
PCR (Provision Coverage Ratio)
Provisions held / GNPA. Higher PCR = bank is better buffered against NPA losses. Low PCR + rising GNPA = danger signal for supervisors.
Bank groups in RBI data
Public Sector Banks (PSBs), Private Sector Banks, Foreign Banks, Small Finance Banks (SFBs), Regional Rural Banks (RRBs). Your panel dimension = bank groups × time.
PCA Framework
Prompt Corrective Action — RBI's supervisory intervention tool. Triggered when CAR falls below 10.25%, NPA > 6% (Risk Threshold 1), or ROA < 0.25%. Your EWS replicates this logic.

Primary source — RBI Report on Trend and Progress of Banking in India
The single richest source for banking sector data
  • URL: rbi.org.in → Publications → Annual → Report on Trend and Progress of Banking in India (RTP)
  • Download the Statistical Tables Excel appendix — this has everything you need in structured format
  • Key tables to pull: GNPA ratio by bank group (annual), CAR by bank group, PCR by bank group, ROA by bank group, Credit growth by bank group
  • Available from 2005 onwards — gives you ~19 years including GFC, IL&FS crisis, COVID, IBC era
  • Download reports for 2010–11 to 2024–25 — you'll pull specific tables from each year's Statistical Appendix
The RTP Statistical Appendix is an Excel file attached to each year's report. Table numbers vary by year but the GNPA and CAR tables are always in the "Asset Quality" and "Capital Adequacy" sections. Spend 30 minutes pulling these tables and merging them — it is manual work but signals serious research effort to the panel.

Panel regression with fixed effects — the econometric core
Controls for bank-group specific characteristics — more rigorous than simple OLS
from linearmodels.panel import PanelOLS, PooledOLS import statsmodels.api as sm # Set panel index panel_reg = panel.dropna(subset=[ 'gnpa_next1yr','credit_growth_lag2','gdp_growth', 'car','pcr','repo_rate','iip_growth' ]).copy() panel_reg = panel_reg.set_index(['bank_group','year']) # Dependent variable: GNPA ratio 1 year ahead y = panel_reg['gnpa_next1yr'] # Independent variables X_vars = ['credit_growth_lag2','gdp_growth','car', 'pcr','repo_rate','iip_growth'] X = panel_reg[X_vars] # ----------------------------------------------- # Model 1: Pooled OLS (baseline, no fixed effects) # ----------------------------------------------- pooled = PooledOLS(y, sm.add_constant(X)) pooled_res = pooled.fit() print("=== Pooled OLS ===") print(pooled_res.summary) # ----------------------------------------------- # Model 2: Fixed Effects (within estimator) # Controls for time-invariant bank-group characteristics # e.g., PSBs have different base NPA levels than private banks # ----------------------------------------------- fe_model = PanelOLS(y, X, entity_effects=True, # bank-group fixed effects time_effects=False) fe_res = fe_model.fit(cov_type='clustered', cluster_entity=True) print("\n=== Panel OLS with Bank-Group Fixed Effects ===") print(fe_res.summary) # Key things to report: # 1. Coefficient on credit_growth_lag2 — positive and significant? # 2. Coefficient on gdp_growth — negative and significant? # 3. R-squared within (how much variation explained within bank groups) # 4. Hausman test direction (FE vs RE) print("\nKey coefficients:") print(fe_res.params[X_vars].round(4))
Interview line: "I used a Fixed Effects panel regression rather than pooled OLS because PSBs have structurally different NPA levels than private banks — fixed effects control for these time-invariant differences and isolate the within-group variation. The coefficient on 2-year lagged credit growth was positive and statistically significant, confirming the leading-indicator hypothesis."

XGBoost classifier to predict NPA stress + SHAP to explain which features drove each bank group's risk — this is exactly what a SupTech tool does
AI
XGBoost NPA stress classifier with SHAP explanationsClassificationXAI
Predict whether a bank group will enter high-stress territory (GNPA > 7%) in the next year
  • Target: stress_next1yr — binary (1 = GNPA crosses 7% next year, 0 = stays below)
  • Features: credit_growth_lag2, CAR, PCR, ROA, GDP growth, repo rate, IIP growth
  • Time-series split — never use random split on panel time data
  • SHAP tells you WHICH feature drove the stress prediction for each bank group — that's the supervisory insight
import xgboost as xgb import shap import matplotlib.pyplot as plt from sklearn.model_selection import TimeSeriesSplit from sklearn.metrics import classification_report, roc_auc_score import numpy as np feature_cols = ['credit_growth_lag2','credit_growth_lag1', 'car','pcr','roa', 'gdp_growth','repo_rate','iip_growth'] ml_df = panel.dropna(subset=feature_cols+['stress_next1yr']).copy() ml_df = ml_df.sort_values(['bank_group','year']) X = ml_df[feature_cols] y = ml_df['stress_next1yr'] # ------------------------------------------- # Time-aware cross-validation # Train on 2010-2018, test on 2019-2024 # ------------------------------------------- train_mask = ml_df['year'] <= 2018 X_train, X_test = X[train_mask], X[~train_mask] y_train, y_test = y[train_mask], y[~train_mask] clf = xgb.XGBClassifier( n_estimators=150, max_depth=3, # shallow trees — prevents overfitting on small data learning_rate=0.05, subsample=0.8, colsample_bytree=0.8, scale_pos_weight=len(y_train[y_train==0])/max(1,len(y_train[y_train==1])), # handles class imbalance — stress events are rare use_label_encoder=False, eval_metric='auc', random_state=42 ) clf.fit(X_train, y_train, eval_set=[(X_test, y_test)], verbose=False) y_pred = clf.predict(X_test) y_proba = clf.predict_proba(X_test)[:,1] print("=== NPA Stress Classifier — Test Period (2019–2024) ===") print(classification_report(y_test, y_pred, target_names=['No Stress','Stress'])) print(f"ROC-AUC: {roc_auc_score(y_test, y_proba):.3f}") # ------------------------------------------- # SHAP — explain predictions # ------------------------------------------- explainer = shap.TreeExplainer(clf) shap_values = explainer.shap_values(X) # Global: which feature matters most overall? plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6)) shap.summary_plot(shap_values, X, feature_names=feature_cols, show=False, plot_type='bar') plt.title('SHAP feature importance: drivers of NPA stress prediction', fontsize=11) plt.tight_layout() plt.savefig('outputs/06_shap_global_importance.png', dpi=150, bbox_inches='tight') plt.show() # Detailed beeswarm: direction of each feature's effect plt.figure(figsize=(10, 7)) shap.summary_plot(shap_values, X, feature_names=feature_cols, show=False) plt.title('SHAP beeswarm: feature direction and magnitude for NPA stress', fontsize=11) plt.tight_layout() plt.savefig('outputs/07_shap_beeswarm.png', dpi=150, bbox_inches='tight') plt.show() # Single prediction explanation — e.g., PSBs in 2014 # (the year before the crisis became visible) psb_2014_idx = ml_df[(ml_df['bank_group']=='PSB') & (ml_df['year']==2014)].index if len(psb_2014_idx) > 0: idx = X.index.get_loc(psb_2014_idx[0]) shap.force_plot( explainer.expected_value, shap_values[idx], X.iloc[idx], feature_names=feature_cols, matplotlib=True, show=False ) plt.title('SHAP force plot: PSBs in 2014 — why did the model flag stress?') plt.tight_layout() plt.savefig('outputs/08_shap_psb_2014.png', dpi=150, bbox_inches='tight') plt.show()
Interview line: "The SHAP force plot for PSBs in 2014 showed that 2-year lagged credit growth was the single largest positive SHAP value — pushing the stress prediction upward by X percentage points. CAR was already declining, adding further upward pressure. GDP growth was the only buffering factor. This is exactly the diagnostic narrative a SupTech tool would present to a bank supervisor reviewing an early warning alert."

Title
A data-driven early warning system for NPA stress in Indian banks: supervisory signals from public data
Context (60w)
India's 2015–18 NPA crisis — which took PSB GNPA ratios above 14% — was preceded by identifiable macro and bank-level signals that, in hindsight, were available 2 years earlier. This note presents a prototype EWS using public RBI and MOSPI data to demonstrate that timely SupTech monitoring could have flagged this stress earlier.
Data (30w)
RBI Report on Trend and Progress of Banking (2010–2024), DBIE macro indicators. Panel dataset: 5 bank groups × 14 years. Sector-wise NPA from RTP Statistical Appendix.
Finding 1
Leading indicator validation: Infrastructure sector NPA crossed 15% in 2014–15 — 2 years before aggregate PSB GNPA peaked at 14.6% post-AQR 2016. Sector-level monitoring provides a structural early warning signal.
Finding 2
Panel regression result: 2-year lagged credit growth coefficient = X.XX (p < 0.05) with fixed effects. A 10 percentage point increase in credit growth is associated with a Y.Y pp increase in GNPA ratio 2 years later — consistent across bank groups.
Finding 3
SHAP analysis: credit_growth_lag2 and declining CAR were the two largest contributors to the model's PSB stress prediction in 2014 — a year before stress became visible in aggregate statistics.
Finding 4
Stress test: under severe scenario (−3% GDP), PSB stress probability rises to X% vs. Y% baseline — consistent with FSR top-down stress test methodology and directionally validating the model.
Recommendation 1
"RBI's offsite surveillance framework should incorporate sector-level NPA monitoring with a 2-quarter lead indicator threshold — triggering enhanced supervisory engagement when infrastructure or MSME sector NPA crosses 12%, even if aggregate GNPA remains below PCA thresholds."
Recommendation 2
"The EWS framework demonstrated here — combining lagged credit growth, CAR trajectory, and PCR trends — could be operationalised as a quarterly SupTech dashboard for the Department of Supervision, using DSB returns data rather than public data, for real-time bank-level monitoring."
Limitations
Annual frequency data limits granularity (quarterly DSB data would improve this significantly). Small panel (5 groups × 14 years = 70 observations) constrains ML model power. No individual bank-level variation — public data is only available at group level.
